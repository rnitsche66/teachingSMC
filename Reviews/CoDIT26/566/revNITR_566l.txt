TITLE:

Magnetorheological Damper Relative Velocity Estimation using a
Takagi-Sugeno Fuzzy Observer with Unmeasured Premise Variables

#_0566

Authors: Kicheol Jeong1, Seongjin Kim1, Hyungjeen Choi1 and
         Hyomin Jin1 - South Korea


**************************************************
Short summary:
**************
Summary (3–6 sentences)

The paper proposes a Takagi–Sugeno (T–S) fuzzy
observer to estimate the relative velocity of a
magnetorheological (MR) damper in a quarter-car
suspension, where the premise (fuzzy) variables
are unmeasurable.

A phenomenological MR damper model and a Bouc–Wen
hysteresis model are used to derive a sector-based
T–S fuzzy representation; Lyapunov-based LMI
conditions are given to guarantee asymptotic
stability of the observer.

Experimental validation is performed on a
quarter-car test rig with sprung/unsprung
accelerometers and an LVDT-derived damper velocity
under sinusoidal road inputs (2–4 Hz) and
different current inputs; results show the
estimator tracks relative velocity robustly, with
transient error increases when discontinuous
current inputs occur.

The authors claim the observer enables practical
MR-damper state estimation using sensors commonly
available on vehicles and supports model-based
fault diagnosis and control development.

Suggestions for improvement (specific, actionable)
**************************************************

Structure and clarity

- Reorganize Section II (models) so each model
  (phenomenological, Bouc–Wen, T–S derivation) is
  a clear subsection with a short roadmap at its
  start; add a compact table of notation to avoid
  symbol overload and repeated explanations.

- Improve readability of equations and figure
references: label key equations (e.g., observer
law, error dynamics, LMIs) and summarize the LMI
conditions in one concise boxed display with brief
interpretation (physical meaning of each
constraint).

Theoretical exposition

- State assumptions explicitly and early (e.g.,
  bounds / Lipschitz constants, sensor noise
  assumptions, availability of
  accelerometers/LVDT) and discuss their physical
  plausibility for on-vehicle deployment.

-Clarify how unmeasurable premise variables are
 estimated (method used to compute premise
 weights) and show sensitivity of stability
 conditions to errors in premise-variable
 estimation.

Experiments and evaluation

-Expand experimental scenarios: include a broader
 set of road profiles (e.g., random/real road
 data, step inputs, and varying amplitudes) and
 disturbances (sensor noise, model mismatch) to
 demonstrate robustness beyond single-frequency
 sine tests.

-Quantify estimator performance with standardized
 metrics (RMSE, maximum absolute error,
 convergence time) and present these across
 scenarios and current inputs in a concise table;
 include confidence bounds or repeated-trial
 statistics.

Comparison and baselines

-Add comparisons with at least one alternative
estimator (e.g., EKF, sliding-mode observer, or a
simpler linear observer) on the same experimental
data to contextualize performance and
computational cost.

- If possible, include a simulation study that
isolates model mismatch and sensor noise to
compare nominal performance and robustness of the
proposed T–S observer vs. baselines.

Discussion

-Expand the discussion section to interpret
 experimental error cases (e.g., why discontinuous
 current causes transient error, how quickly the
 observer recovers) and discuss practical
 implications: required sampling rates, sensor
 bandwidth, and how the method would integrate
 with MR control algorithms.

-Discuss limitations and potential failure modes
 (e.g., large model parameter drift, heavy
 nonstationary excitations) and suggest mitigation
 (adaptive gains, robustified LMIs, or online
 model update).

References and reproducibility

- Ensure references are complete and formatted
 consistently; add recent relevant works on
 MR-damper observers and state estimation for
 semi-active suspensions (especially EKF/SMO/T–S
 observer comparisons) to better situate the
 contribution.

- Improve reproducibility by summarizing key
  parameter values (mass, stiffness, Bouc–Wen
  parameters, observer gains, LMI solver used) in
  an appendix or supplementary table and (if
  possible) provide code snippets or pseudocode
  for the observer implementation.
