TITLE:

Nonlinear Boost Compensation for PD-Controlled
Self-Balancing Robot Stabilization


#_0378

Authors: Tawfik et al


**************************************************
Short summary:
**************
The paper presents a hybrid PD control scheme with
a bounded nonlinear “boost” augmentation for a
two-wheeled self‑balancing robot implemented on a
low‑cost Arduino Nano 33 IoT platform.


Key elements are:
*****************
- A reduced-order linearized plant model is
  derived and experimentally validated (open‑loop
  instability checks and parameter
  identification). The linear model is used for
  pole placement and baseline PD design.

- The controller is PD near equilibrium but adds a
  bounded boost term when the tilt error magnitude
  exceeds a threshold. The boost is designed to
  overcome practical actuator non‑idealities
  (motor dead‑zone, stiction, saturation) while
  preserving local linear stability.

- Dead‑zone compensation, command saturation, and
  a complementary filter (fusing accelerometer and
  gyroscope) are used in the implementation. The
  control loop runs at ≈38 Hz.

- Stability arguments: local asymptotic stability
  (linear PD region) and BIBS reasoning when the
  boost and saturation are active are
  provided. Root‑locus and second‑order
  approximations are used to justify gain choices.

- Simulations (MATLAB/Simulink) and experiments on
  the physical robot show recoverability from a
  ∼20° push: experimental settling time ≈0.44 s,
  RMS tilt error ≈3.60°, and 96% time within ±5°
  stability band. Performance is compared with
  other literature controllers (LQR, PID, fuzzy
  PID).

- Practical implementation issues identified: IMU
  noise and gyroscope drift, complementary‑filter
  phase lag (~10 ms), motor dead zone (~35 PWM
  counts), battery voltage sag under peak current,
  motor/drivetrain inertia and driver
  delay. Authors propose future work including
  integral action with anti‑windup, encoder‑based
  position control, and Kalman filter state
  estimation.


Suggestions for improvement (specific, actionable)
**************************************************

1. Strengthen the theoretical stability analysis
************************************************

- Provide a formal proof that includes the
  nonlinear boost term, e.g., a Lyapunov argument
  showing forward invariance of a neighborhood and
  convergence properties, rather than relying
  primarily on BIBS heuristics. If possible,
  quantify the enlarged region of attraction.

- Explicitly state assumptions (actuator bounds,
  sampling, sensor delays) and show robustness
  margins (gain and phase margins, input
  disturbance bounds).

2. Improve modeling of implementation non‑idealities
****************************************************

- Extend the reduced‑order model to include
  dominant nonidealities impacting performance:
  motor dynamics (electrical and mechanical time
  constants), driver delay, drivetrain inertia and
  friction, battery internal resistance producing
  voltage sag. Even low‑order augmentations
  (first‑order motor dynamics and input dead‑zone
  model) can close the gap between simulation and
  experiment.

- Calibrate and report parameter identification
  method and uncertainties (e.g., measured motor
  time constant, dead‑zone threshold variance).

3. Enhance estimator and sensor treatment
**************************************************

- Replace or augment the complementary filter with
  a Kalman filter (or unscented/extended KF if
  nonlinearities retained) to improve state
  estimation and to provide covariance‑based
  uncertainty measures. Show quantitative
  improvement in angle noise and drift compared to
  the complementary filter.

- Report sampling jitter, exact sensor sampling
  timings, and time alignment between IMU and
  control loop. Consider resampling or
  timestamping to reduce phase errors.

4. Address integral action and steady‑state offset
systematically
**************************************************

- The PD controller produces a steady‑state offset
  for step disturbances. Implement PI/D with
  anti‑windup (or conditional integration) and
  show stability implications with the boost and
  saturation present. Provide experimental
  comparisons (RMS, settling time, peak command).

5. Improve experimental evaluation and reproducibility
**************************************************

- Add more systematic tests: multiple repeated
  trials with statistical metrics (mean ± standard
  deviation for s ettling time, RMS error, time in
  band) and confidence intervals.

- Test across operating conditions: different
  battery charge levels, payloads, floor friction,
  and temperature to demonstrate robustness.

- Provide a clearer table of hardware and software
  configuration (exact IMU firmware settings,
  sampling rates, filter α, PWM frequency, motor
  gearbox ratio, wheel radius, battery type and
  nominal voltage).

6. Compare more fairly with other controllers
**************************************************
- When comparing to LQR, PID, fuzzy PID in
  literature, match experimental conditions where
  possible or replicate baseline controllers on
  the same hardware to ensure fair comparison. If
  not possible, discuss differences in hardware
  and measurement methods more explicitly.

7. Refine controller design choices and tuning rationale
**************************************************
- Explain how boost threshold and amplitude were
  selected (optimization, grid search, heuristic)
  and evaluate sensitivity of closed‑loop
  performance to these parameters. Provide tuning
  guidelines for practitioners.

- Consider a smooth (continuous) boost transition
  (with tunable smoothing parameter) to reduce
  control discontinuity and potential chattering
  near the threshold; then analyze effects on
  performance and stability.

8. Improve presentation clarity
**************************************************
- Clean up figures and captions: include units on
  axes, add legends, and ensure simulated vs
  experimental plots use identical scales for
  direct visual comparison.

- Present mathematical notation and equations with
  clear definitions of all symbols and variable
  units; move lengthy implementation details
  (e.g., Arduino pinouts) to an appendix or
  supplementary material.

- Provide a concise summary table listing all
  controller parameters used in experiments (Kp,
  Kd, boost threshold, boost amplitude, dead‑zone
  compensation).

9. Consider more advanced control variants as
benchmarks
**************************************************
- Evaluate a controller with adaptive gain
  scheduling or a model predictive controller
  (MPC) (even a simple linear MPC) as a benchmark
  for performance vs computational cost on
  low‑cost hardware.

- Investigate use of encoder feedback (as authors
  propose) and show comparative gains in
  disturbance rejection and steady‑state accuracy.

10. Safety and real‑world considerations
**************************************************
- Discuss safety limits, e.g., maximum recoverable
  disturbance, actuator heating under sustained
  high commands, and strategies for safe failure
  (e.g., soft stop, safe shutdown) when outside
  region of attraction.

- Provide guidance for field tuning and
  diagnostics (how to detect dead‑zone drift over
  time, recommended recalibration intervals).
