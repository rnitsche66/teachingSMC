The newly uploaded PDF document titled "Comparative Evaluation of PD,
Computed-Torque, and Adaptive Computed-Torque Control for Trajectory
Tracking on a Franka Panda Manipulator" presents a reproducible ROS 2
Humble–CoppeliaSim benchmark comparing three control
strategies—Proportional–Derivative (PD), Computed Torque Control
(CTC), and Adaptive Computed Torque Control (ACTC)—for trajectory
tracking on a Franka Panda robotic manipulator under partial torque
actuation (only joints 1–3 are commanded).

Key points include:

The study executes a two-phase factorial campaign totaling 120 runs
with multiple repetitions, assessing performance across trajectory
types, speed regimes, parameter mismatches, and sudden payload
changes.

Results show that CTC improves tracking accuracy and reduces control
effort and saturation relative to PD under nominal conditions.

ACTC demonstrates superior robustness compared to CTC under parameter
mismatches and payload disturbances, achieving substantially lower
tracking errors and faster recovery times (e.g., median recovery times
of about 0.7–0.9 seconds after payload steps, compared to longer or
censored recovery times for CTC).

Torque RMS and saturation ratios are lower for CTC and ACTC compared
to PD, with ACTC slightly higher during adaptation phases.

The benchmark uses realistic robot parameters and applies rigorous
statistical analyses, including ANOVA and Kaplan–Meier survival
analysis for recovery times, ensuring reproducibility and meaningful
comparison.

The paper discusses practical recommendations, limitations due to
partial actuation, and plans for future work involving full 7-DOF
torque control and hardware validation.

Overall, the document provides a thorough, experimentally validated
comparison of these control methods on a widely used robotic platform,
highlighting the benefits of adaptive computed torque control in
realistic scenarios involving modeling uncertainties and disturbances.
