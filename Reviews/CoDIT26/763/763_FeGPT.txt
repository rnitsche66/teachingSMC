Summary

Motivation:

The paper addresses limitations of conventional rigid-frame multirotor
UAVs in disturbance rejection and maneuverability. It proposes using
compliant (flexible) structural elements in the frame to obtain
enhanced lateral disturbance resistance and richer actuation through
passive geometric/material compliance rather than additional
actuators.

Problem statement:

Derive a scalable, first-principles modeling and control framework for
an octocopter whose arms are flexible rods modeled as chains of
viscoelastic single-DoF joints. Capture the coupled rigid–flexible
dynamics, design/implement a baseline controller, and assess stability
and disturbance rejection in simulation.

Methods:

Mechanical design concept: an octocopter with four arms; each arm is a
flexible rod composed of n alternating viscoelastic joints (odd joints
allow torsion; even joints allow bending). Each arm terminates in a
rotor pair (two rotors per arm) with inward-tilted thrust vectors to
bias forces toward the center of mass.  Kinematics and dynamics:
derive full rigid–flexible kinematics and equations of motion using
the rod-chain approach and Euler–Lagrange formalism. Build mass matrix
M, Coriolis/centrifugal terms V(q, q̇), gravity G(q), generalized input
forces Qgen and damping Qdamp. Jacobians for link velocities and
moments are derived symbolically; elastic and gravitational potential
energies are defined.  Simulation implementation: custom MATLAB code
implementing the N-DoF model (6 rigid-body DOF of base + 4n structural
DOF + 8 rotor DOF treated as internal states). Parameter set and
simulation scenarios (hover, wind disturbance with a sustained 5 N
horizontal load) are used.  Control: a PID-based controller acting on
the rigid-body base states with aggregate feedback from arm
bending/torsion states; rotor thrusts and torques produce
forces/moments.  Main results:

The derived analytical framework provides explicit, modular
expressions for all dynamical quantities for scalable numbers of
joints per arm.  Hover simulations show the PID controller stabilizes
the vehicle in ideal hover with minor altitude deviations.  Under a
simulated sustained 5 N horizontal disturbance, the vehicle exhibits
significant deviations in altitude and lateral position, indicating
limited disturbance rejection under the tested baseline controller.
The paper concludes that the modeling and computational framework is a
solid foundation for future adaptive/robust control strategies
tailored to flexible multirotor systems.  Conclusions: The authors
present a comprehensive first-principles modeling framework for
flexible-arm octocopters, demonstrate feasibility in simulation, and
highlight the need for more advanced control (adaptive/robust) and
experimental validation to fully exploit the potential benefits of
structural compliance.

Key strengths (5–10)

Clear and relevant motivation: addresses an important
problem—improving multirotor disturbance rejection and maneuverability
via structural compliance without added actuation complexity.

First-principles derivation: thorough Euler–Lagrange derivation of
rigid–flexible dynamics, including Jacobians, mass matrix, and
potential energies.

Scalable modeling approach: rod-chain formulation with modular
expressions that scale with n (number of links per arm), facilitating
parametric studies.

Transparent implementation: custom MATLAB implementation described
(parameters provided), which supports reproducibility (within limits
of the manuscript).

Insightful design concept: alternating bending/torsion joints and
inward-tilted rotor thrust vectors are sensible design choices to
create richer actuation and center-directed reaction forces.

Identification of limitations: authors honestly report
disturbance-rejection shortcomings under baseline PID control and
point toward future adaptive/robust controllers.  Concrete weaknesses
or gaps (5–10)

Lack of experimental validation: results are purely simulation-based;
no hardware experiments or bench tests are reported to confirm model
fidelity.

Limited control design: only a baseline PID on rigid-body states is
implemented; no advanced controllers (adaptive, robust, or
model-based) are tested despite the coupled dynamics.  Sparse
quantitative performance metrics: results are presented qualitatively
(plots) but lack systematic quantitative evaluation (e.g., RMS
tracking error, settling time, control effort, frequency response).

Disturbance scenarios limited: only one magnitude/direction of
sustained horizontal load (5 N) tested; no range of wind profiles,
gusts, or parameter variations (mass/inertia/elasticity) examined.

Unclear sensitivity to modeling assumptions: reduced-order single-DoF
joints and viscoelastic parameters are assumed but the effect of these
simplifications on accuracy is not analyzed.

Controller uses only aggregate flexible-state feedback: control
architecture seems ad hoc and may not exploit the full
high-dimensional state for improved performance.  No formal stability
or robustness analysis: theoretical guarantees (e.g., Lyapunov
stability, input-to-state stability) for the closed-loop system with
flexible dynamics are absent.  Presentation/readability issues: some
algebra and derivations appear dense; figure captions and numerical
tables could be clearer and better summarized.  Limited comparison to
prior art: while references are cited, quantitative or structured
comparison to existing fully-actuated or seesaw designs is missing.
Parameter justification: choices for elasticity, damping, link mass
distribution, rotor tilts, and PID gains lack extensive justification
or tuning methodology.  Practical, prioritized improvement actions

Priority 1 — Strengthen validation and controller design

Add experimental validation (bench or scaled prototype tests). Even
simple static deflection tests and single-arm actuation experiments
would help validate the viscoelastic link model and stiffness/damping
choices.  Implement and compare at least one model-based controller
that leverages the derived dynamics (e.g., inverse dynamics + feedback
linearization, nonlinear model predictive control, or adaptive
control) and compare with the baseline PID.  Perform systematic
disturbance tests (gusts with varying amplitude and frequency,
multi-directional loads) and report quantitative metrics (RMS error,
max deviation, recovery time, control input energy).

Priority 2 — Quantify sensitivity and robustness

Conduct sensitivity analysis on critical parameters: number of links
n, link stiffness/damping ratios, rotor tilt angle, and mass/inertia
distribution. Report how tracking and disturbance rejection change.
Provide robustness margins (gain/phase margins if linearized around
operating points) or perform Monte Carlo simulations over parameter
uncertainties.

Priority 3 — Formal analysis and exploitation of flexibility

Attempt a reduced-order modal decomposition (e.g., obtain dominant
flexible modes) and design a controller that explicitly accounts for
modal interactions; this can reduce controller dimension while
capturing essential flexible dynamics.  Provide at least local
stability analysis (e.g., linearization at hover and eigenvalue
analysis) to support simulation observations.  Consider hybrid control
architectures that combine a rigid-body outer loop with inner
flexible-mode dampers (collocated damping or active vibration
suppression).

Priority 4 — Improve experiments, comparisons, and metrics

Compare performance against relevant alternatives in literature
(tilted-rotor designs, rotating seesaw, fully-actuated octocopters)
using identical simulation conditions to highlight relative
advantages.  Report standardized metrics and include tables
summarizing results for clarity (tracking errors, actuator commands,
computation time).  If hardware is not feasible, provide extensive
high-fidelity simulation scenarios (CFD-coupled wind loads, stochastic
gust models).

Priority 5 — Writing and presentation improvements

Reorganize derivations: move very lengthy algebraic expansions to an
appendix or supplementary material; keep main text focused on physical
interpretation and key expressions.  Improve figures and captions: add
labeled schematics showing joint numbering, rotor tilt angles, and
definitions of reference frames; include clearer time-series plots
with legends and quantitative annotations.  Clarify parameter choices
and PID tuning process in a table with units and rationale.  Add a
short "limitations and future work" section that explicitly lists the
assumptions and how they will be addressed.

Suggested small edits and clarifications (concise)

Define concisely the configuration vector q and its partitioning
(base, flexible joints, rotors) early and use consistent notation
throughout.  When reporting simulation results, always accompany plots
with numeric summaries (peak error, steady-state error, energy/cost).
If possible, provide code repository link or pseudocode for the
simulation pipeline (note: do not add links unless requested).


Final note: The manuscript delivers a valuable, carefully derived
modeling framework and a compelling design concept. Prioritizing
experimental validation, more sophisticated controllers that exploit
the derived model, sensitivity/robustness analyses, and clearer
presentation will significantly strengthen its scientific
contribution. Cross-verify numeric claims and parameter selections
when moving to hardware or comparative studies.
